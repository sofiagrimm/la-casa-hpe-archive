1960s — The Founding Decade
Origins
1969
FoundingOrganizing
La Casa Cultural established at Yale
Puerto Rican and Chicano students pressure the Yale administration for a dedicated cultural space, marking the official founding of La Casa Cultural.
+ Read more
Context

Amid the national Chicano Movement and the civil rights era, Yale students demanded institutional recognition. La Casa's founding coincided with the establishment of other cultural houses at Yale, including the Afro-American Cultural Center.

Students organized sit-ins, wrote petitions, and met directly with administrators to demand a permanent space and staff.

1969
Coalition
Solidarity with AACC founding
La Casa students stand alongside the founding of the Afro-American Cultural Center, building the cross-cultural alliance that would define both institutions for decades.
+ Read more

The parallel founding of both cultural centers in 1969 established a model of institutional solidarity. Joint meetings, shared programming, and reciprocal advocacy became a hallmark of both organizations.

1970s — Consolidation & Culture
Building roots
1970
CulturalOrganizing
First annual Baile de La Casa
La Casa establishes its first annual dance celebration, a tradition that continues to the present day as one of the most anticipated cultural events on Yale's calendar.
+ Read more

The Baile quickly became more than a social event — it was a public assertion of Latine cultural presence at an institution that had long ignored it. Musical genres featured included salsa, cumbia, and merengue.

1972
AcademicAdvocacy
Demands for Chicano/Latino Studies curriculum
Students formally petition Yale's curriculum committees for courses addressing Chicano, Puerto Rican, and broader Latin American experiences in U.S. history and literature.
+ Read more

The demands mirrored movements at universities across the country. La Casa students compiled syllabi, identified potential faculty, and presented a full proposal to the Dean's office. Individual courses began appearing in the mid-1970s.

1977
FoundingMilestone
Move to permanent home at 301 Crown St.
La Casa moves into its permanent three-story red brick house at 301 Crown Street — the physical anchor of the center for the next half century.
+ Read more

The 19th-century building offered a kitchen, library, and lounge — creating a genuine home. Students organized renovation work parties to prepare the space. The address became synonymous with the Latine experience at Yale.

1978
Cultural
Named for Julia de Burgos
La Casa is formally named "La Casa Cultural de Julia de Burgos" in honor of the Puerto Rican poet and activist, linking the center's mission to her legacy of resistance and identity.
+ Read more

Julia de Burgos (1914–1953) was a celebrated Puerto Rican nationalist poet whose work centered themes of womanhood, political resistance, and diasporic identity. Her selection was a deliberate statement about the intersectional values of the center.

1980s — Expansion & Resistance
Political awakening
1981
Advocacy
Campaign for undocumented student support
La Casa students organize among the first advocacy campaigns at any Ivy League institution for protections and financial support for undocumented students.
+ Read more

Following Plyler v. Doe (1982), which established K-12 rights for undocumented children, La Casa pushed Yale to extend institutional support further, focusing on counseling resources and legal referrals.

1984
OrganizingCoalition
Meso-American solidarity organizing
La Casa spearheads cross-campus protests and forums in solidarity with Central American peoples facing U.S.-backed conflicts in El Salvador, Nicaragua, and Guatemala.
+ Read more

The Sanctuary Movement brought Central American refugees to New Haven and Yale's campus. Students worked alongside New Haven community organizations and Yale's Divinity School.

Sanctuary Movement
Central American solidarity
1986
AcademicMilestone
First Latinx faculty hire tied to student demands
Years of student advocacy result in Yale's first tenured Latinx faculty appointment directly tied to La Casa's documented curriculum demands.
+ Read more

Students collected signatures, presented research on peer institution demographics, and organized a faculty forum. The appointment marked the first time La Casa's formal advocacy produced a lasting structural change in Yale's faculty composition.

1989
Cultural
La Casa 20th anniversary celebration
The twentieth anniversary brings alumni back to 301 Crown St. for a multi-day celebration featuring the first oral history documentation project.
+ Read more

The anniversary generated the first significant archival collection of La Casa's history — photographs, printed programs, and recorded interviews with founding-era students. This material forms the core of the HP&E digital archive.

1990s — Identity & Expansion
New voices
1991
Academic
Council on Latin American & Iberian Studies established
Lobbying by La Casa students and faculty allies results in a formal interdisciplinary program for Latin American studies.
+ Read more

The new program provided formal institutional structure for courses La Casa students had demanded since the early 1970s. La Casa's directors co-chaired the founding advisory committee.

1994
CoalitionCultural
Solidarity with EZLN and Zapatista uprising
La Casa hosts forums and organizes campus responses to the Zapatista uprising in Chiapas, deepening connections between Yale students and indigenous rights movements.
+ Read more

The January 1, 1994 EZLN uprising aligned with NAFTA's implementation. La Casa students contextualized this within hemispheric economic justice debates. Yale law students and La Casa co-organized teach-ins across spring semester.

1997
Advocacy
Yale dining workers' strike coalition
La Casa students play a central role in supporting Yale's HERE Local 34/35 dining workers' labor actions, building the town-gown solidarity model that would define future activism.
+ Read more

Many Yale dining workers were members of New Haven's Latine and Black communities. La Casa's solidarity work forged enduring relationships between cultural houses and New Haven's working-class communities of color.

2000s — Community Deepening
New century
2006
AdvocacyCoalition
Marcha por la Dignidad — New Haven immigration march
La Casa students join over 20,000 marchers in New Haven's response to proposed federal immigration legislation, one of the largest civic demonstrations in Connecticut's history.
+ Read more

Sparked by H.R. 4437, which would have criminalized undocumented immigrants, La Casa organized student transportation and coordinated with New Haven's Unidad Latina en Acción (ULA).

2007
Milestone
New Haven ID Card campaign — first in the U.S.
La Casa students collaborate with ULA and New Haven city officials to launch the Elm City Resident Card — the nation's first municipal ID card accessible to undocumented residents.
+ Read more

The Elm City Resident Card became a national model for municipal sanctuary policy, adopted by over a dozen U.S. cities. Yale students staffed enrollment events and provided legal resources.

2009
Organizing
DREAM Act advocacy at state and federal levels
La Casa students testify before the Connecticut General Assembly and organize Yale delegations to Washington in support of federal DREAM Act legislation.
+ Read more

Multiple La Casa-affiliated students shared personal testimony in Hartford and at congressional offices. The advocacy culminated in Connecticut passing its own DREAM Act in 2011, allowing in-state tuition for undocumented students.

2010s — Naming, Reckoning & Growth
Institutional change
2012
AcademicMilestone
Yale Latinx Studies Program launched
Following years of advocacy, Yale formally institutionalizes Latinx Studies as a program with dedicated faculty, courses, and a departmental home.
+ Read more

The program fulfilled demands first articulated in 1972. La Casa provided archival evidence of student organizing to support accreditation and helped recruit inaugural faculty.

2012
Advocacy
DACA response and campus organizing
La Casa coordinates Yale's immediate response to the DACA announcement, providing legal resources and community support within 48 hours.
+ Read more

La Casa partnered with Yale Law School's immigration clinic to help New Haven community members access the new policy. The rapid response became a model cited by other Ivy League cultural centers.

2015
OrganizingAdvocacy
Next Yale movement — renaming Calhoun College
La Casa joins the Next Yale coalition demanding the renaming of Calhoun College, marking one of the most visible intersectional campaigns in Yale's modern history.
+ Read more

The Next Yale movement included demands for greater faculty diversity, a review of building namings, and expanded support for students of color. Calhoun College was renamed Grace Hopper College in 2017.

Present tense

BLM solidarity and police reform coalitions
